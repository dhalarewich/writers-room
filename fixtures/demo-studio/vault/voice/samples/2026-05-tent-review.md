# Thirty-four nights in the Halcyon 2

The zipper died on night nine.

Not the tent's fault, it turned out, but mine: I'd been running it gritty since a sandstorm outside Hanksville and never once cleaned the coil. The Halcyon's zipper is a YKK #5, same as everything else in this category, and a #5 will forgive you about a week of that treatment. Mine got nine days. I cleaned it with a toothbrush at a picnic table in Torrey and it has worked without complaint for twenty-five nights since, which tells you most of what you need to know about how this shelter fails: slowly, honestly, and usually because of something you did.

The fabric is where the money went. The fly is a 20-denier sil/PeU that has taken two hailstorms and a full night of wet snow at 9,400 feet, and the only mark on it is a scuff from a trekking pole tip that was my fault too. At 210 grams lighter than the tent it replaced in my kit, the Halcyon buys its weight back in sleep. I have not once woken up wet in it.

Here's the part the spec sheet hides: the vestibule zipper placement means you will drip on your own footbox every time you open the door in rain. Every time. I tested four door-opening techniques and the driest one still put a tablespoon of water inside. If you camp mostly in the Cascades, that tablespoon is your nightly tax.

Who shouldn't buy it: anyone who pitches on rock ledges. The stake-out geometry needs eight honest points and gives you nothing if it can't have them.

The zipper that died on night nine is the same zipper that's on the four-hundred-dollar version of this tent. Clean your coils.
